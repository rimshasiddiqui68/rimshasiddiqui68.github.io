Project Background

Understanding mortality is about more than numbers—each data point represents a human life and a story. Analyzing causes of death can be emotionally weighty, yet it plays a critical role in informing global health policies, allocating healthcare resources, and addressing preventable health crises. Mortality data serves as a mirror reflecting the medical, social, and economic conditions of societies across time and place.

For this project, I worked with a comprehensive dataset from the Our World in Data platform, which spans 202 countries from 1990 to 2019. The dataset includes detailed records of deaths categorized by 31 different causes, such as heart disease, cancer, respiratory infections, neonatal disorders, and interpersonal violence. This 30-year longitudinal dataset offers rich opportunities to uncover trends, regional disparities, and shifts in global health priorities.

Executive Summary

The aim of this project was to clean, organize, and visualize this extensive mortality dataset in a meaningful way. Using Power BI, I created an interactive dashboard that enables users to identify the most lethal diseases, observe how death patterns have evolved over the years, and compare regional health outcomes—ultimately supporting better data-driven decisions in public health.

This analysis focuses on global mortality trends across 202 countries from 1990 to 2019, highlighting shifts in causes of death and their geographic distribution. From 54 million total deaths globally, non-communicable diseases (NCDs) accounted for 74%, with cardiovascular diseases and cancers being the top culprits. Notably, deaths in China and India saw significant increases, while neonatal disorders and infectious diseases have declined over time. Additionally, countries with large populations and regions like Europe and Africa face the highest mortality rates, emphasizing the importance of tailored health interventions.

Key Insights

  • Leading Causes of Mortality: Cardiovascular diseases and neoplastic diseases (cancers) have remained the top causes of death globally over the years.
  • Changes in Mortality Trends by Country:
    • In China, deaths from cardiovascular diseases and neoplasms nearly doubled, rising from 2.4 million in 1990 to 4.6 million in 2019.
    • In India, the death toll increased from 1.2 million in 1990 to 2.6 million in 2019.
    • The increase has been slower in countries like Russia and the United States, though chronic disease deaths continue to rise steadily.
  • Growth in Cancer Mortality:
    • In China, cancer-related deaths grew from 1.5 million in 1990 to 2.7 million in 2019.
    • In the United States, cancer deaths increased from 0.5 million to 0.77 million over the same period.
  • Declining Role of Neonatal Disorders: Neonatal disorders, once among the top causes of death, have significantly declined in prominence globally.
  • Top Countries by Total Deaths:
    • China: 265 million deaths (average of 8.8 million/year).
    • India: 238 million deaths (average of 7.94 million/year).
    • Followed by the United States and Russia.
  • Global Death Trends: Global deaths increased from 43 million in 1990 to 54 million in 2019, showing a steady rise in mortality worldwide.
  • Cause Breakdown:
    • Non-communicable diseases (NCDs): 74% of all deaths.
    • Communicable diseases: 14%.
    • Violent deaths: 1.3% from suicide, <1% from interpersonal violence.
  • Top 5 Non-communicable Diseases Responsible for Mortality:
    • Cardiovascular diseases: 448 million deaths.
    • Neoplasms (Cancers): 230 million deaths.
    • Chronic respiratory diseases: 105 million deaths.
    • Cirrhosis: 37 million deaths.
    • Diabetes mellitus: 31 million deaths.
  • Continents with the Highest Total Deaths:
    • Europe: 574 million deaths.
    • Africa: 540 million deaths.




Conclusion

This analysis highlights a global shift toward non-communicable diseases (NCDs) as the primary causes of death, especially in densely populated countries like China and India. While deaths from neonatal and communicable diseases have declined, chronic illnesses such as cardiovascular disease, cancer, and diabetes are rising steadily. The data emphasizes the urgent need for preventive healthcare, better chronic disease management, and stronger healthcare systems to address growing health disparities and reduce avoidable mortality worldwide.

Recommendations

  • Focus on Prevention: Encourage early detection and healthier lifestyles to reduce deaths from chronic diseases like heart disease, diabetes, and cancer.
  • Raise Awareness: Launch public campaigns targeting risk factors such as smoking, poor diet, and physical inactivity.
  • Strengthen Healthcare Access: Improve infrastructure and make healthcare more affordable, especially in low- and middle-income countries.
  • Support High-Risk Groups: Prioritize care for vulnerable populations, including the elderly and uninsured.
  • Encourage Global Collaboration: Share best practices and resources across countries to tackle common health challenges more effectively.
